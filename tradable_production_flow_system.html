Production flow + tradability framework
BEA SAGDP × BEA I-O tables (2022 benchmark) × Census exports 2024. Assumptions documented inline.
BEA SAGDP industry groups classified by tradability. Three tiers: fully tradable (T), partially tradable (PT), and non-tradable (NT). Classification follows Spence & Hlatshwayo (2012) and BEA I-O export data.
BEA SAGDP industryNAICSClassTradability rationaleI-O export share of gross outputIntermediate share of output
Classification rule: T = sector where goods/services routinely cross borders and prices are set internationally. PT = significant export capacity but majority of output consumed domestically. NT = output consumed at point of production; near-impossible to trade.

Assumption A1: Construction is classified NT despite some cross-border engineering contracts — the physical output is location-bound. Assumption A2: Wholesale trade is classified PT because it intermediates internationally traded goods. Assumption A3: Finance is classified PT (goods exports = $284B in 2022 I-O, real estate near zero) — split to separate FIRE into Finance (PT) vs Real Estate (NT) when data permits.
Export Coverage Ratio = goods exports ÷ tradable GDP. Values >100% signal service-sector economies where goods exports understate full global exposure. Filter and sort by any column.
Interpretation: ECR < 30% → goods exports significantly understate global exposure; expect large services trade or re-export adjustment. ECR > 80% → goods exports closely track tradable output; economy is production-tethered to global trade. ECR > 100% → pass-through artifact (Louisiana, New York gold).
Assumption A4: Tradable GDP = manufacturing + agriculture + mining + wholesale trade (50%) + information (40%) + professional services (25%) + finance (20%). These partial-sector weights follow Spence & Hlatshwayo (2012) methodology. Full BEA SAGDP2N disaggregation would sharpen these weights.
Production flow model: for each tradable sector, total gross output splits into exports, domestic final demand, and intermediate use. Ratios sourced from BEA I-O Use Table 2022 (sector level). State-level export shares from Census 2024.
All 50 states classified by export utilization ratio (EUR = exports ÷ tradable production). Three archetypes: export-driven, supply-chain hub, domestically consumed. Search or sort.
Export Utilization Ratio (EUR) = goods exports ÷ estimated tradable gross output. Tradable gross output estimated as tradable GDP ÷ (1 − intermediate share), where intermediate shares are BEA I-O sector averages. EUR > 35%: export-driven. EUR 15–35%: supply-chain hub (high intermediate use). EUR < 15%: domestically consumed tradable sector.

Assumption A5: State-level intermediate shares are proxied from national BEA I-O sector averages. State-level I-O tables exist for ~30 states via IMPLAN/RIMS but are not publicly standardized across all 50.

Consistency checks — where new metrics validate or challenge existing signals
Validates: Indiana's high EUR (pharma exports ÷ pharma production ≈ 55%) confirms the existing high alignment score (+21pp) and GEPS position. The ECR (goods exports ÷ tradable GDP ≈ 82%) is high, confirming goods are closely tracking tradable output — no hidden services gap to correct for.

Challenges: California's GEPS v1 was penalized as "domestically focused." ECR (4.5% goods intensity ÷ ~35% tradable GDP share = ECR ~13%) reveals the extent of the gap, but the services trade correction (BEA ITA: CA services exports ~$60–80B estimated) would add ~3–4% to effective intensity, partially rehabilitating its global position. California moves up 1–2 ranks under GEPS v2.

New signal: Supply-chain hub classification (EUR 15–35%) identifies states like Ohio and Illinois whose tradable sectors primarily supply domestic intermediate demand rather than exporting. These states have strong alignment scores but are more exposed to domestic demand cycles than their export figures suggest — a risk not captured in GEPS v1.
